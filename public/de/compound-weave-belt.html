STRUCTURAL PRINCIPLE

How the Double-Spiral Structure Works - and Why Density Matters

A standard balanced weave belt uses one spiral coil per pitch unit - left-hand and right-hand spirals alternating across the belt width, connected by straight cross rods. The compound weave belt uses two interlocked spiral coils per pitch unit. The result is approximately twice the number of wire contact points per unit area, producing a measurably denser mesh surface.

This density difference has practical consequences. The smaller effective mesh opening allows smaller products to be conveyed without falling through, dropping, or deforming under their own weight. The denser surface distributes load more evenly across the product contact area - reducing contact pressure and surface marking on soft products such as dough, pastries, biscuits, and formed food items. It also provides better drainage uniformity in washing and air-cooling applications, where a coarser mesh would allow uneven water or airflow distribution.

~2x mesh density vs standard balanced weaveTwo spiral coils per pitch unit - doubled wire contacts per unit area. Smaller effective opening without increasing cross rod count.
Zero lateral drift - same as balanced weaveLeft-hand and right-hand spiral forces balance each other. The compound weave belt tracks straight under load without the belt drifting to one side - same fundamental geometry as balanced weave, applied at double density.
Diagonal measurement - production quality controlBoth belt edges must be the same length. Diagonal measurement is performed during production to verify geometric consistency. Diagonal deviation causes drift and skip under drive - eliminated at the production stage.
Denser surface - more even load distributionThe higher wire contact density reduces per-contact load - less surface marking, better support for soft or fragile products, more uniform drainage or airflow through the belt surface.
Heavier than standard balanced weave - same widthMore wire per unit area means more weight per metre. Confirmed against drive system load capacity before specification is finalised.

WIRE TYPE AND CONFIGURATION

Three Wire Types - Four Special Configurations

YIYI Mesh Belt produces compound weave belts in three wire types and four structural configurations - each matching a specific application requirement. Wire type is not a preference; it determines whether the belt will perform correctly under the drive and load conditions of the intended application.

WIRE TYPE 01
Soft Wire (Annealed / WEA)

Standard wire condition for friction-driven compound weave belts. More flexible - easier to form into the tight double-spiral geometry. Used in baking, food processing, and washing line applications where the belt is driven by friction against the carrying surface or edge.

Applications: baking ovens · washing lines · cooling conveyors · oil filtration
WIRE TYPE 02
Spring Wire (High-Tensile)

Required for gear-driven compound weave belts. Higher tensile strength supports the engagement stress of positive sprocket drive without progressive spiral deformation at the gear contact zone. Using soft wire in a gear-driven application causes spiral deformation after sustained operation - eliminated by specifying spring wire as standard for all gear-driven orders.

Required for: positive gear drive · side sprocket drive · heavy continuous duty
WIRE TYPE 03
Flat Wire (Compressed Cross-Section)

Round wire compressed to a flat cross-section (e.g. 1.5mm x 1.25mm from 1.0mm round). The flat face increases the wire-to-product contact area - reducing contact pressure per unit area on soft product surfaces. Used where round wire contact marks are unacceptable. Confirmed from actual YIYI production orders.

Applications: soft food products · delicate surface contact · marking-sensitive applications

CONFIGURATION 01
Straight Shaft
Standard cross rod configuration - straight rods pass through the spiral coils at 90 degrees to the belt running direction. Used in most friction-driven compound weave belt applications. Confirmed from production data: 304 stainless steel, widths 250mm - 2,320mm, 3-rod to 6-rod configurations.
CONFIGURATION 02
Curved Shaft / Crank Rod
Cross rods are formed with a curved or cranked profile rather than straight. The curved geometry provides an additional mechanical interlock between rod and spiral - improving rod retention and reducing the risk of rod pull-out under high tension or lateral load. Confirmed from actual YIYI production orders: SUS304 curved shaft compound weave, widths 1,560mm - 2,320mm.
CONFIGURATION 03
Flat Wire Spiral
Spiral coils formed from flat-section wire - round wire compressed to a rectangular cross-section before spiral forming. The flat face of the wire increases the belt surface contact area. CONFIGURATION 04
Chain-Net Integrated

Used where positive drive is required alongside a dense mesh carrying surface. FAILURE ANALYSIS

Three Common Compound Weave Belt Failures - and How YIYI Prevents Them

Most compound weave belt failures occur at predictable points in the production or installation chain. Understanding the failure mode is the first step to preventing it.

⚠ Failure Mode 01
Belt Drifts or Runs Off Track After Installation
Cause: diagonal asymmetry - the belt was not produced with equal-length edges. The geometric imbalance creates a net lateral force under tension, pulling the belt to one side. Discovered after installation, not visible before.
✓YIYI Production Standard
Diagonal Measurement During Production - Both Edges Confirmed Equal
Diagonal measurement is part of the production inspection for every compound weave belt at YIYI Mesh Belt. Both edges must be the same length and both diagonals must be equal - verified before the belt is assembled. Drift is eliminated at the factory stage, not corrected at the installation site.
⚠ Failure Mode 02
Spiral Deformation at Gear Engagement Zone
Cause: soft wire used for a gear-driven belt. Repeated engagement stress at the sprocket contact point progressively deforms the spiral coils - causing pitch irregularity, engagement noise, and belt jump after sustained operation. Not visible immediately after installation.
✓YIYI Production Standard
Spring Wire Mandatory for All Gear-Driven Compound Weave Belt Orders
YIYI Mesh Belt specifies spring wire (high-tensile) as standard for all gear-driven compound weave belt orders. The belt specification is confirmed before production - wire type is not left to default. A first-piece running test with the matched sprocket is also performed before batch production is released.
⚠ Failure Mode 03
Belt Surface Contaminated - Cleaning Agent or Water Residue
Cause: insufficient cleaning after weld-area acid pickling - residual acid or cleaning solution left on the mesh surface, or surface not fully dried before packing. Causes discolouration, corrosion, or contamination of handled product at the installation site.
✓YIYI Production Standard
Surface Must Be Fully Cleaned and Sun-Dried Before Packing - No Exceptions
YIYI Mesh Belt production records explicitly require: cleaning after acid pickling, no residual cleaning solution on mesh surface, no water marks, and fully dried before boxing. Surface condition inspection is part of the pre-shipment QA - not checked only on request.

A compound weave conveyor belt uses a double-spiral (herringbone) structure - two interlocked spiral coils per pitch unit instead of one. The result is approximately twice the mesh density of a standard balanced weave belt, producing a denser, more closed carrying surface with smaller mesh openings. This makes it suitable for applications requiring better product support, finer drainage, or controlled airflow that a standard single-spiral belt cannot provide. The same left/right spiral balance principle ensures zero lateral drift during operation.
Both use alternating left-hand and right-hand spirals - the key difference is mesh density. This gives the compound weave belt a denser surface, smaller effective mesh opening, and improved support for small or soft products. The tradeoff is greater weight per metre and slightly less flexibility - relevant when specifying drive system load capacity. Both run straight without lateral drift under the same balanced geometry principle.
